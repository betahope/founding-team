# Bottom-up TAM: the full method (and optional web research)

Founders struggle with the market-size question more than any other. They reach for a top-down number ("the market is $50B, we will get 1%") because it is easy. Readers distrust it instantly. A bottom-up TAM, built from real buyers and a real price, is what earns credibility.

The quick four-step version is in `questions.md`. Read this file when a founder needs the full method, is segmenting buyers, cannot source a buyer count, or wants you to research the numbers. The method applies to any program. Techstars asks for it explicitly, but a bottom-up number is the stronger answer everywhere.

## The method, done properly

A bottom-up TAM is built from the ground up: count the real buyers, multiply by what each realistically pays per year.

**Step 1: Define the buyer precisely.** Tie this to the Ideal Customer Profile from the target-audience question. The buyer is the specific person or organisation that actually pays. "Restaurants" is loose. "Full-service restaurants in the US" is countable. If the ICP is not nailed down yet, fix that first. A fuzzy buyer makes every number downstream fuzzy.

**Step 2: Count the buyers from real data.** How many of that buyer actually exist? This has to come from a credible source (government statistics, industry associations, a census of businesses, market reports), not a round guess. This is the number founders most often invent, and the easiest for a reader to verify or refute.

**Step 3: Set a realistic annual revenue per buyer (ACV).** Annual Contract Value is what one buyer pays you per year. Base it on actual pricing or a defensible planned price, not a hopeful one. If you bill monthly, annualise it ($100/mo becomes $1,200 ACV).

**Step 4: Segment when buyers differ.** If buyers split into groups that pay very differently (enterprise versus SMB, multi-location versus single), do not blend them into one average. Compute each segment separately: buyers in segment × ACV for that segment. This is more honest and usually more persuasive.

**Step 5: Multiply and sum.**
- Single segment: TAM = ACV × number of real buyers
- Multiple segments: TAM = the sum of (buyers in segment × ACV in segment) across segments

**Step 6: Sanity-check against a top-down figure.** Cross-check the result against any top-down market number you can find. If your bottom-up number is wildly larger than the known industry size, the buyer count or the price is wrong. Adjust until it is defensible.

## Worked example

Annual subscription of $1,200. US: 749,404 restaurants, so roughly $749.4M. Europe: 1.5M restaurants, so roughly $1.8B. The strength here is that both the buyer count and the price are concrete and checkable, not "the food-tech market is huge."

## Common mistakes to flag

- **Top-down hand-waving.** "1% of a $50B market." Readers discount it entirely. Force the bottom-up build.
- **Counting everyone.** Using the whole population instead of real, reachable buyers who have the problem and will pay.
- **Fantasy pricing.** An ACV the buyer would never actually agree to.
- **One blended ACV** when segments clearly pay different amounts.
- **No source for the buyer count.** The single most common fatal flaw.
- **A TAM that is too small.** For a VC-backed program, a small TAM is a dealbreaker. If the honest number is small, widen the buyer definition (adjacent segments, more geographies) rather than inflating the price.

## How this lands in the answer

The full math is the founder's working analysis. The answer itself is short (on Techstars, the market-size box is about 400 characters). Build the full logic, then compress to: the revenue model in one line, the bottom-up headline (buyers × price = $X), and the reachable near-term slice. The full table belongs in the deck or data room, not the form.

## Optional: offer to research the numbers

The buyer count and the ACV are the two inputs founders most often guess, and both are researchable. When a founder reaches the market-size question, offer to ground them:

> "The two numbers that make or break this answer are how many buyers actually exist and how much each would pay per year. Want me to dig into both with a web search and build the TAM from cited figures? You can correct anything that looks off."

If the founder says yes, and web search is available in this environment, run several targeted searches rather than one:

1. **Buyer count.** Search for the size of the buyer population: number of businesses or establishments in the category and geography, industry-association membership counts, government or census data. Run separate searches per geography or segment if the TAM spans more than one. Prefer original sources (government, trade associations, reputable market reports) over aggregators.
2. **Revenue per buyer.** Search for pricing benchmarks of comparable products, typical contract or subscription values in the category, or what incumbents charge, to pressure-test the founder's planned price.
3. **Top-down cross-check.** Search for the reported overall market size, to sanity-check the bottom-up total in Step 6.

Then present the bottom-up build with each input attributed to its source, show the arithmetic, and ask the founder to confirm or adjust before it goes in the answer. Make clear these are research-based estimates, not guarantees. The founder owns the final numbers. Never invent a figure to fill a gap. If research is thin, say so and use a clearly labelled range.

If web search is not available in this environment, say so plainly and work from the founder's own figures, flagging which ones a reader will want a source for.
