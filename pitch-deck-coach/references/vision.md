# Vision in pitch decks

Long-form guidance on what vision is, where it goes, and how to write it. SKILL.md covers the rules. This file covers the worked detail.

---

## What vision is

Vision is the world the company is trying to create. The destination. Not the product the company has shipped, not the market it operates in, not the next milestone.

A good vision line answers: if this company succeeds completely, what is different in the world?

Vision shows the reader where the company is heading. Without it, the deck is a tactical product pitch. The reader gets what the company does today but not why anyone should care over the next ten years. Investors and accelerators screen for ambition. A deck with no visible vision reads as "this founder is not thinking big enough", even when the founder is.

## What vision is not

Founders, and AI tools drafting for founders, regularly collapse vision into adjacent concepts. The differences matter when critiquing.

**Vision is not the tagline.** The tagline is the marketing line on the cover. It tells the reader what the company does today, in one breath. Vision tells the reader where the company is going. They are different jobs. A cover line like "Customer discovery any founder can properly do" is a tagline. A closing line like "A world where building what nobody wants kills 5% of startups, not 42%" is a vision.

**Vision is not the mission.** The mission is what the company does every day to make the vision real. "We help founders run honest customer interviews" is a mission. "A world where founders stop building things nobody wants" is a vision.

**Vision is not the positioning.** Positioning is what the company is versus its competitors. "The customer discovery tool for non-technical founders" is positioning. It belongs near the competition slide or the value prop. It is not vision.

**Vision is not a corporate statement about the company.** "We will be the leading platform for X" is not vision, it is ambition expressed as PR copy. Vision is about the world or the user, not about the company.

All three (tagline, mission, positioning) can appear in the same deck as the vision. They are not interchangeable. Watch for decks (and AI drafts) that put a tagline on the closing slide in a giant font and call it the vision. That is the most common failure mode.

## Where vision goes

Use this as a decision tree. The basics from the founder (audience, format, length) determine placement.

### Emailed decks

Default: vision is the closing slide.

The reader's last impression should be the destination. The closing slide is also where the bookend trick works best, because the reader has the opening problem stat fresh enough to feel the loop close.

Move vision earlier only if there is a specific reason. For example, an emailed deck for a partner conversation may put a "shared destination" line near the value prop, then close on contact details. That is fine. Pure investor and accelerator emailed decks should close on vision.

### Live pitch, 5 to 10 minutes

Two options.

**Vision opens.** Right after the problem, as the "why we exist" beat. Use this when the founder wants to set ambition before showing proof, especially with weaker traction. The pitch reads as: "here is the world we are trying to build, here is why now, here is what we have done so far, here is the ask."

**Vision closes.** Safer default. The pitch reads as: "here is the problem, here is what we built, here is the proof, here is the world this leads to." The closing beat carries the emotional weight.

Do not do both. Vision appearing twice dilutes it.

### Live pitch, 3 minutes

Usually no room for a dedicated vision slide. Vision folds into either the opening problem framing or the closing line. The slide deck may not name it explicitly, but the spoken pitch has to. Still required, just compressed into a single sentence inside another beat.

### Live pitch, 1 minute

Vision is a single line of the spoken pitch, typically the closer. Not a slide of its own. The deck may be three or four slides total. The vision line is delivered verbally as the founder lands the close.

### Accelerator decks

Strongly prefer closing on vision. Pre-accelerators and accelerators are explicitly looking for "is this founder thinking big enough." A weak or missing vision reads as a soft rejection signal even when the rest of the deck is good. Programs want to see that the founder has thought past the next six months.

### Investor decks

Depends on the founder's leverage.

- Strong traction. Open on traction (or open on the problem and reach traction by slide 3). Vision closes as the emotional payoff. The reader has already been convinced this works. The vision tells them how big it gets.
- Weak traction or pre-product. Vision opens. The reader needs a reason to keep going before they hit the lighter traction slide. Set the ambition first so the rest of the deck reads in that frame.

### Partner or customer decks

Vision is about a shared destination, not the founder's solo ambition. "The world we want to build with you" or "what this partnership makes possible." Often near the value prop or the close, rarely the opener.

## Strong and weak vision lines

Examples below are illustrative. Founders should write their own, grounded in what they actually believe.

### Strong

> A world where building what nobody wants kills 5% of startups, not 42%.

Why it works: specific, measurable, talks about the world (not the company), bookends an opening fact ("42% of startups fail building things nobody wants"). Reads in one breath at anchor size.

> A world where every restaurant owner can read their next health inspection score before the inspector walks in.

Why it works: specific to the user, names a concrete future state, no buzzwords. Anyone in the room can picture it.

> A world where the cost of running a customer interview drops from a week to an afternoon.

Why it works: quantified, user-centred, specific to the founder's actual product space. The reader knows exactly what changes.

### Weak

> Empowering founders to unlock their full potential.

Why it fails: buzzwords (empower, unlock), no specifics, talks about an abstract group rather than a recognisable future state. Could be any company.

> We will be the leading platform for customer discovery.

Why it fails: about the company, not the world. "Leading platform" is corporate-speak. The reader cannot picture anything.

> Transforming how startups build products.

Why it fails: buzzword (transform), no measurable change, no user, no world. AI drafts default to this shape constantly. If a vision line uses "transform," "revolutionise," "reimagine," or "unleash," it is not yet a vision line.

> Customer discovery any founder can properly do.

Why it fails as a vision: this is a strong tagline but not a vision. It tells the reader what the company does today. It does not name the world that exists if the company succeeds. If this line is on the closing slide as the vision, the deck is missing its vision.

## The bookend pattern

The bookend is when the opening problem stat and the closing vision line share a number or a fact, with the closing line projecting the change forward.

Example:

- Slide 2: "42% of startups fail building things nobody wants."
- Closing slide: "A world where that number is 5%, not 42%."

When it works: the reader feels the deck close the loop they did not realise was open. The number does the work without explanation. The closing slide does not need to argue, only to land.

When it is forced:

- The opening stat is not actually the problem the company solves. The reader notices the mismatch.
- The closing number is invented or unsupported. If the founder cannot defend "why 5%, not 8% or 20%," the bookend collapses under questioning.
- The bookend is too long. A bookend that needs three lines to land is not a bookend, it is a paragraph.
- The product cannot plausibly move the number. Bookending a 42% failure rate against a tool that addresses one cause of failure overstates the claim. Pick a narrower stat the product can actually move.

The bookend is the most effective vision pattern when it works. It is not required. A standalone vision line with no numerical bookend is also fine if it lands.

## Vision and the founder's actual conviction

A vision line the founder does not believe reads as PR copy. The reader can tell.

This shows up in two ways.

**The vision is bigger than the founder's actual ambition.** The founder put a "change the world" line on the deck because they thought a deck needed one, but if asked over coffee they would describe the company as "a useful tool for a specific kind of user." The investor or reviewer who probes will find the gap fast. Pull the vision back to what the founder genuinely believes, then make that version specific and recognisable.

**The vision is smaller than the founder's actual ambition.** The founder is genuinely trying to change something big but the deck only describes the next feature. This is more common with engineers and first-time founders who are uncomfortable saying ambitious things out loud. The fix is to ask the founder, in plain language, "if this works, what is different in the world in ten years?" The answer is usually the vision. They just needed permission to say it.

When coaching or critiquing, do not write the vision line for the founder without first checking what they actually believe. A founder cannot defend a vision line they did not write or genuinely hold. They will be asked about it. The line has to be theirs.
